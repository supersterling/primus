## Design as Differentiation

Most software design exists to make new technology understandable and accessible — to help people get started with something unfamiliar. That's valid, and it's about 99% of what gets made.

Design as differentiation is something else. It's the kind of design you see in fashion, automotive, or consumer goods — where design is used to make something *worth wanting*, not just usable. The product doesn't need to explain itself. It has a point of view.

> "We are intentionally trying to innovate on design as differentiation — not design as a way of making a new technology more approachable, which is a very valid form, but to me that's been 99% of software design."
> — Andy Allen

### What this looks like in practice

- You're not reinventing a category — you're reinventing a familiar one with a strong aesthetic opinion
- The interface itself is the value proposition, not just a wrapper around a feature
- You can see the creator's taste in the product
- Users don't need onboarding docs — they *feel* what the product is in the first 30 seconds

### What it rules out

- Following design trends because they're current
- Adding AI or new technology because it's expected
- Optimizing for conversion or growth above experience
- Making something that "scales" at the expense of feel

### The fashion analogy

In fashion, experimental work from top studios slowly trickles down — tier by tier — until eventually it's a shirt at Walmart. Students of fashion know who Alexander McQueen and Virgil Abloh are. They know who the taste-makers are.

Software design is inverted: students admire the Walmarts and Targets — the big tech apps — because they don't know who the Virgil Ablohs of software are. Design as differentiation is about being that tier-one voice, even at small scale.

### Rule

**Don't add a feature because you can. Add it because it makes the experience more distinctly itself.**

If a new capability would blur your product's point of view, cut it. The Not Boring camera app dropped a clever Holga-inspired wind-snap gesture entirely because, after the first few uses, it got in the way of what the product actually was.
