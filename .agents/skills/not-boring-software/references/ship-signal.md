## The Ship Signal

Knowing when to ship is one of the hardest decisions in product work. Andy Allen's signal: **when you're sick of it, but others still react to it.**

> "There's a moment where you get totally sick of your project. I usually think that's about the time you're kind of done with it. But when I'd show it to others, they would have a reaction. That's a good moment. That's when I switch into marketing mode."
> — Andy Allen

### Why this works

Creators lose the ability to see their own work clearly after prolonged exposure. What feels stale and obvious to you is often surprising and delightful to someone seeing it fresh. The gap between your boredom and their reaction is the signal.

If you're tired of it *and* others are also flat — keep going. The product isn't done.
If you're excited *and* others are flat — you may be too close. Step back.
If you're tired *and* others still react — **ship it.**

### What happens when you switch to marketing mode

Andy describes this as reconnecting with the project as an outsider. You start thinking about:

- The name
- The branding and iconography
- How you'd describe it to someone who's never heard of it
- The story you'd tell about why it exists

This outsider framing often rekindles excitement — you fall in love with it again through fresh eyes. That's the energy you want to ship with.

### Rules

**Don't ship because you're excited. Ship because others are.** Your own excitement is not a reliable signal after months of close work.

**Show it to people outside the project regularly.** Not for feedback on what to change — to take the temperature of their reaction. That's your data.

**When the reaction is there, move fast.** The window between "ready to ship" and "I kept tweaking it" is where a lot of good work goes to die.
