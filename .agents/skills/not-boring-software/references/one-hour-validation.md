## One Hour Validation

Most ideas die not because they're bad, but because they never get tested. They live in your head, growing in complexity, until the gap between the idea and the effort to explore it feels too large to cross.

The fix: one focused hour.

> "If you just carve out an hour sometime — just spend one hour. You can get a lot done. You can almost validate the first step of almost any idea in one hour. That usually tells you about 80% of what you need to know."
> — Andy Allen

### What one hour can produce

- A rough sketch of the layout or interaction
- A Figma rough with the core screens
- A Lego or paper model of the form
- A working prototype of one gesture or animation
- A clear answer to: *is there something here?*

The output doesn't need to be shareable. It needs to answer the question.

### What "validation" means here

Not: does this idea have product-market fit?
Not: is this technically feasible at scale?

Just: **is there something here worth exploring further?**

That's 80% of what you need to know, and you can get it in an hour if you commit to it.

### Rules

**Timebox ruthlessly.** One hour. Not "this afternoon." Set a timer. The constraint forces you to reduce the idea to its most testable core.

**Pick the medium that answers the question fastest.** Sketch if it's about layout. Code if it's about feel. Lego if it's about form. Don't default to Figma out of habit.

**Don't pre-judge whether the hour is "worth it."** The ideas that seem too small or too weird are often the most interesting ones. The hour is cheap. Spend it.

**If the hour doesn't tell you anything — the question was wrong, not the idea.** Try a different angle in the next hour.
