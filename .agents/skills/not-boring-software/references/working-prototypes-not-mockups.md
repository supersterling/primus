## Working Prototypes, Not Mockups

When evaluating whether an interaction idea has merit, build the real thing — not a Figma flow. A Figma prototype can tell you if something *looks* plausible. Only a working build tells you if it *feels* right.

> "By prototype, I mean actually build it — a working app. We gave ourselves two weeks to build the camera control winding idea as a real app, played with it, and knew within that session whether it had something."
> — Andy Allen

### Why this matters

Interactions that look great in Figma regularly feel wrong in use — and interactions that seem too subtle to bother mocking up can turn out to be the whole product. The only way to know is to use it with real hardware, real physics, real latency.

For Not Boring, the Holga-inspired wind-snap camera gesture seemed compelling in concept. Two weeks of real prototyping revealed it was fun for the first use, then cumbersome. That's a decision that could not have been made in Figma.

### When to skip the mockup entirely

- When the idea is primarily about gesture, motion, or haptics
- When the interaction depends on real-world timing or physics
- When you're evaluating whether something is *delightful*, not whether it's *understandable*
- When the core question is feel, not layout

### Rules

**Give yourself a fixed window — two weeks or less — to build a working prototype of any serious interaction idea.** If it can't be roughed out in that time, the idea is too complex to validate yet. Simplify the idea first.

**A working prototype doesn't need to be polished.** It needs to answer one question: does this feel right? Everything else is noise.

**Don't present Figma flows as interaction validation.** They're layout validation. These are different things.
