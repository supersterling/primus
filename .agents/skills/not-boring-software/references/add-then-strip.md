## Add Until Bloated, Then Strip Back

The best version of a product rarely looks like the first idea. The real creative process is permissive on the way in and ruthless on the way out. Add everything that seems interesting. Then remove everything that doesn't belong.

> "We kept adding to it and eventually it got very bloated. The winding gesture was fun, but after the first couple uses it felt cumbersome. So we threw out the whole initial idea that started it. We found the real thing by stripping everything else away."
> — Andy Allen

### The Not Boring camera arc

1. Started with a Holga-inspired wind-snap gesture tied to the iPhone's camera control button
2. Built it. Loved it. Added more.
3. Got bloated. The core gesture started to feel like a gimmick.
4. Cut everything — including the original idea.
5. Focused on what remained: the raw image + LUT-based color grading
6. That became the product.

The final camera app has nothing in common with the idea that started it. That's not failure — that's how you find the real thing.

### Why "add first" works

- You can't know what doesn't belong until you've tried it
- Constraints applied too early kill good ideas before they have a chance
- Bloat makes it obvious what the core actually is — by contrast
- The act of adding things you later cut isn't waste; it's research

### When to cut

- When a feature is "fun the first time" but fades
- When something requires explanation before it feels right
- When removing it would make the product *more itself*, not less
- When it was the original idea — that's not a reason to keep it

### Rule

**Be permissive going in, ruthless going out. The original idea is not sacred.** The Not Boring camera app threw out the entire concept that started the project. What it became was better because of that willingness.

Don't protect your early ideas. Protect what's worth keeping — which you often won't know until you've built and cut enough to see it clearly.
