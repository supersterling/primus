## Prototype in the Real Medium

The fastest tool for a given problem isn't always the digital one. Get off the computer. The goal is to feel the thing — its weight, texture, ergonomics, and form — before you commit to building it in code.

> "I've gotten a lot more into 3D printing. I printed a version of the camera app just to hold it — to feel the texture of the controls, the placement, the ergonomics. We did a Lego skin for our weather app and I literally just built it in Lego first."
> — Andy Allen

### The right tool for the question

| Question | Medium |
|---|---|
| Does this layout communicate the right thing? | Sketch or Figma |
| Does this feel right to hold and interact with? | 3D print |
| Does this visual language work at component scale? | Physical model (Lego, clay, paper) |
| Does this animation timing feel right? | Blender or code prototype |
| Does this gesture feel natural? | Working app prototype |

### Why physical prototyping works

- **Ergonomics can't be felt on screen.** A button that looks right in Figma may feel wrong in your hand.
- **Form reads differently as an object.** 3D printing an interface forces you to think about it as a product, not a layout.
- **Physical constraints clarify decisions.** Lego has a grid. Working in it forces choices about proportion and density that screen tools let you ignore.
- **It's faster for spatial/tactile questions.** Sketching a 3D form by hand is faster than modeling it in software.

### Rules

**Don't use Figma to answer questions Figma can't answer.** Figma is a sketchbook — great for layout, hierarchy, and quick ideation. It cannot tell you how something feels to use.

**Treat Figma documents like sketchbooks, not deliverables.** Don't organize them like a design system. Throw stuff down fast. If someone looked at your Figma files and they were messy — that's fine. That's the point.

**Use the cheapest medium that answers the question.** A quick pencil sketch before opening any software. A Lego prototype before writing a shader. A 3D print before finalizing an interaction model.
